Chapter 1: The Problem of Social Causality and Critique of Existing Approaches

1.1. The Relevance of the Study: Social Institutions as Emergent Phenomena

Social institutions—money, property, or borders—possess a remarkable property: their existence depends on what we think about them. The collective representation “this paper is money” does not merely describe the world but actively shapes it, endowing the paper with real causal force. This “ontological effect”—the capacity of epistemic constructions to generate new social facts—constitutes one of the central and most challenging problems in social ontology.

The problem lies not only in the complexity of the phenomenon but also in the state of research attempting to explain it. Philosopher Mark Turner compares social science to pre-Darwinian biology (Turner, 2001): despite an abundance of empirical data and particular theories, it lacks a unifying principle that would show precisely how stable social entities emerge from simple interactions.

Both animals and humans coordinate their behavior strategically. At least, it can be described as such by game theory. However, some game-theoretic models of human and animal coordination are formally indistinguishable. Consider two butterflies competing over a mating spot, where one is the “owner” and another is “intruder” of the spot. How do they resolve this dispute? As research shows, they mostly take into account who occupied the spot first (Davies, 1978). This makes each butterfly capable of following one of the conditional strategies:

  • “if I am an owner of this spot, I will fight over it whatever it takes”
  • “if I am an intruder, I will show hostility but will not escalate and eventually retreat”.

Now consider a similar situation in humans. Two tribes graze their cattle on different sides of a dried riverbed. When there was a river, they both knew that there is another tribe over the river and it is in their best interest not to trespass. After the river has dried, they kept using a physical marker (dried riverbed) to construct a more abstract conditional strategy “if the land is to the South (or North for another tribe) from the riverbed, graze the cattle” (Guala & Hindriks, 2015). Thus, they resolve a potential conflict around their property in a similar way by following a conditional strategy.

In both cases, the agents have conditional strategies, but what is hidden is the nature of conditionalization. In the first case, it relies solely on environmental features and in the second, it relies on shared normative beliefs. Butterflies coordinate on order of precedence and do not “think” about it, while we humans not only think but, if described game-theoretically, base the very interaction and its strategies on our representations of others’ actions and beliefs. So, the difference is in what agents condition their strategies on.

The thing which agents condition their strategies on is called correlation device in game theory. And in this dissertation, I investigate the cognitive evolution of correlation devices in naturalistic social ontology: how agents, throughout the evolution, learned to coordinate on increasingly complex correlation devices — from simpler environmental cues like “occupied spot” or “opponent’s size” to shared normative beliefs represented as rules like “North to the river is other tribe’s property, so I do not graze the cattle there”.

Although it might look like the tribe members condition their actions purely on a physical marker of a dried riverbed just like animals would do, it is not the case. A physical marker such as a dried riverbed, as I will argue, scaffolds a deeper epistemic structure involving common reason to believe which land belongs to whom. It encodes information so that agents coordinate not on the riverbed itself but on the associated normative rules. Therefore, it is a correlation device abstracted from a physical signal like “opponent’s size” or even “dried riverbed” with the help of cognitive representation.

From a formal point of view, the problem is almost invisible: regardless of the complexity of correlation device, whether it is “opponent’s size” or “shared beliefs about each other’s property”, the payoffs of the game in the normal form stay the same. What is changing, though, is the informational structure of environment in which the game is played. This structure affects the nature of correlation devices used in the game, either simpler and “flat” or more complex and abstracted.

1.2. The Classical Dichotomy in Social Ontology

The classical dichotomy in social ontology offers two responses. On one hand, analytical approaches, represented by John Searle’s constructivist program (Searle, 1995) and developed by Brian Epstein (Epstein, 2015), describe the logical structure of social facts through formulas of the form “X counts as Y in context C”. However, they do not explain how such “agreements” acquire causal force in the material world. Sally Haslanger’s normative constructivism (haslanger2016?) speaks of correcting social categories to achieve justice, making ontology dependent on political goals and distancing it from realism.

On the other hand, the realist program, most prominently and fully represented by Francesco Guala’s theory of rules-in-equilibrium (Guala, 2016a), shows how stable patterns of behavior can emerge and be maintained through simple rules of action coordination. However, such realism reduces the normativity and ontological status of social facts to behavioral regularity—a regulatory rule “If X, do Y”, eliminating the specificity of the social as ontologically distinct from everything else. Philosopher of science Don Ross characterizes this split as a confrontation between analytical and scientific social ontology, where the former proceeds from the method of conceptual analysis, and the latter from empirical research and the logic of inference to the best explanation (IBE) (Ross, 2023).

Both approaches, despite their explanatory power, describe ready-made static constructions—either an objective social world or its permanent reproduction. Analytical social ontology describes the “magic” of the social but cannot naturalize it, while naturalistic social ontology eliminates the “magic” but loses the specificity of the social itself. The key question remains outside the brackets: what mechanism makes the transition from physical fact to social fact possible?

1.3. Guala’s Rules-in-Equilibrium Theory: A Unified Framework

Francesco Guala’s rules-in-equilibrium theory (RiE) represents a significant advance by integrating the normative and causal dimensions of social conventions with classic social ontology of Searle (Searle, 1995). The theory offers a unified framework that synthesizes two influential traditions in the philosophy of social institutions: the “institutions-as-rules” approach and the “institutions-as-equilibria” approach (Guala & Hindriks, 2015).

Within this theory, social institutions are conceptualized as sets of regulative rules which are conditional strategies of the form “if X do Y” that coordinate behavior by creating correlated equilibria (CE) in strategic interactions. Unlike earlier models that treat institutions either as mere conventions or as constitutive rules, Guala’s framework emphasizes the causal role of CE sustained by agents’ conditional strategies as beliefs. This approach is particularly valuable because it provides a rigorous and philosophically nuanced account of how social order can emerge and persist without presupposing idealized rationality or collective intentionality as much contemporary social ontology does (Jankovic & Ludwig, 2018).

The theory conceptualizes social institutions as sets of regulative rules, representing conditional strategies of the form “if X, then Y”, which coordinate behavior by creating CE in strategic interactions. This approach elegantly solves the problem of dualism in social ontology, however, it leaves open a key question: how such equilibria and the underlying conditional strategies emerge from simpler forms of coordination observed in animals.

1.4. The Core Problem: Mixed Models of Agency

The main problem identified through the analysis of existing materials lies in the mixing of two different models of agency in Guala’s theory:

  1. Classical game theory: assumes fully rational agents with the capacity for common knowledge and strategic thinking
  2. Evolutionary game theory: uses more realistic agents with limited cognitive abilities, following simple reinforcement learning rules

This mixing creates conceptual uncertainty about the nature of cognitive architectures necessary for the existence of epistemic conditional strategies that underlie human social institutions.

The tension lies in the conflation of underlying assumptions about the properties of agents playing a game from different strands of game theory. When explaining the emergence of institutions, Guala uses evolutionary game theory and the concept of ESS to explain how animal conventions might evolve into human institutions. When explaining stability, Guala appeals to classical game theory and the concept of CE to explain why existing institutions are stable.

This mixing creates theoretical tension, since evolutionary game theory assumes agents with limited cognitive abilities, while the concept of CE requires the presence of common knowledge and representations.

1.5. Insufficient Attention to the Evolution of Correlation Devices

Guala’s theory does not pay due attention to the evolution of correlation devices themselves. Within RiE, it is assumed that correlation devices (such as physical markers, social norms, linguistic rules) exist exogenously and are used by agents to coordinate their actions. However, the question of how such devices evolve evolutionarily from simpler forms of coordination remains unanswered.

The thing which agents condition their strategies on is called correlation device in game theory. And in this dissertation, I investigate the cognitive evolution of correlation devices in naturalistic social ontology: how agents, throughout the evolution, learned to coordinate on increasingly complex correlation devices — from simpler environmental cues like “occupied spot” or “opponent’s size” to shared normative beliefs represented as rules like “North to the river is other tribe’s property, so I do not graze the cattle there”.

1.6. The Problem of “Externalism”

Critics point out that Guala’s theory suffers from “externalism”—insufficient consideration of the material and psychological reality of institutions (Rabinowicz, 2018; Roversi, 2021). The theory focuses on formal equilibrium structures, missing the material artifacts, cultural practices, and psychological mechanisms that support the functioning of institutions.

By formalizing the emergence of belief-mediated coordination within agent-based and evolutionary game-theoretic models, the thesis addresses critiques that Guala’s theory is overly externalist or insufficiently accounts for the material and psychological reality of institutions. It provides a detailed account of how rule-like character of institutions can be grounded in evolved cognitive capacities for decoupled representation, thus offering a more robust naturalistic ontology of institutions that integrates both their behavioral and normative dimensions.

1.7. Formulating the Research Problem

The main research problem of the dissertation is formulated as follows:

How can epistemic conditional strategies, dependent on agents’ internal beliefs about others’ behavior, evolve from reactive, non-epistemic conditional strategies based on physical environmental asymmetries through ecological and evolutionary processes of cognitive niche construction that can be rigorously modeled within game-theoretic frameworks with agent-based modeling?

This hypothesis includes several interrelated claims:

  1. Reactive conditional strategies that respond directly to environmental cues without involving internal representations constitute the evolutionary antecedents of more complex, belief-mediated strategies. Such reactive strategies are widespread in non-human animals and form the baseline for social coordination.

  2. Through repeated interactions and selective pressures favoring successful coordination and cooperation, agents develop internal epistemic states like beliefs or representations that mediate their conditional strategy choice. These epistemic states enable agents to predict and respond flexibly to the behavior of others, rather than merely reacting to fixed cues.

  3. The emergence of these epistemic strategies gives rise to proto-normative attitudes towards what actions are appropriate or required in given social contexts. These proto-normative beliefs are the cognitive precursors to full-fledged normative systems.

  4. The entire process can be modeled using agent-based simulation, demonstrating the plausibility of emergence of epistemic correlation devices without presupposing more advanced cognitive constructs like collective intentionality or explicit sanctioning mechanisms.

  5. This transition is not merely conceptual but can be computationally investigated through agent-based simulations that capture the dynamics of the cognitive evolution of correlation devices represented as ecological cues of different layers of abstraction.

This problem solves a key gap in Guala’s theory and provides a naturalistic explanation of the emergence of human social institutions from animal conventions.

1.8. Theoretical Framework and Interdisciplinary Foundations

This dissertation draws on a rich and interdisciplinary theoretical foundation that spans social ontology, philosophy of science, evolutionary biology and cognitive science. Key components include:

1.8.1. Guala’s Rules-in-Equilibrium Theory

Guala’s framework offers a philosophically grounded account of social institutions as CE sustained by agents’ conditional strategies (Guala & Hindriks, 2015). By integrating the normative and causal dimensions of institutions, this theory strives to provide a parsimonious naturalistic ontology of social order that avoids taking metaphysically-laden constitutive rules or collective intentionality as primitives. The present research builds on this foundation by exploring the evolutionary origins of the conditional strategies that sustain these equilibria.

1.8.2. Evolutionary Game Theory

The formal modeling of strategic interactions and their evolution over time is central to this study. Foundational work by Lewis (2008), Maynard Smith (1982), Skyrms (2010) and Gintis (2009) has demonstrated how cooperation and coordination can emerge through evolutionary processes. This dissertation extends these insights by focusing specifically on the evolution of epistemic, belief-mediated strategies from reactive ones, using CE as an analytical tool.

1.8.3. Philosophy of Biology

Understanding the evolution of internal representations and belief systems is critical for distinguishing reactive from epistemic strategies. Sterelny (2003, 2012, 2021) has emphasized the role of decoupled representation in cognitive evolution of humans. Millikan’s teleosemantic theory (millikan1984?) provides a naturalistic account of mental representation grounded in biological function which serves a foundation for Sterelny’s theory. Another important source is Godfrey-Smith’s Environmental Complexity Thesis (Godfrey-Smith, n.d.; Godfrey-Smith, Sternberg, & Kaufman, n.d.), which states that the very function of cognition is navigating in informationally rich environments and producing adaptive response enhancing one’s own fitness. These perspectives inform the conceptualization of epistemic conditional strategies as belief-mediated in the current thesis.

1.8.4. Social Ontology and Philosophy of Social Science

The metaphysical and normative implications of treating social conventions as natural kinds are informed by the work of Boyd (boyd1999?), Searle (1995), and contemporary debates on the naturalization of normativity. Guala’s RiE provides a reference point for situating the present research within ongoing philosophical discussions being “a central hub” and synthesizing major findings across the subdisciplines of philosophy of science.

1.9. Novelty and Contribution

The main novelty (which is also a difficulty for comprehending it) of this dissertation lies in providing causal mechanisms of evolution of complex belief-based correlation devices which lie “behind” the game structure: the agents, their strategies and payoffs stay the same throughout the evolution. What changes is the nature of the correlation device on which the agents coordinate and follow conditional strategies.

Formally, both animals and humans use some “signal”, or environmental asymmetry, be it “opponent’s size” for the former and traffic lights for the latter. But the underlying epistemology of these signals in both cases is what matters most in this work.

Beginning with the foundational models of coordination by David Lewis (Lewis, 2008), Brian Skyrms (Skyrms, 1994, 2010) and others, and extending with scrutiny of Guala’s game-theoretic social ontology, the analysis leads to introduce a crucial distinction between ontic correlation of strategies — ecological, cue-driven mechanisms of coordination leading to CE, and epistemic correlation — cognitive, representation-based processes that enable agents to interpret and respond to social cues which lead to epistemic CE. Formally, these are the same, as agents still play stable and optimal conditional strategies, but what is “hidden” is the nature of correlation device which signals each player a preferable strategy.

This project does three things:

  1. In social ontology, it resolves an inner tension, which tries to explain the stability of social institutions by appealing to equilibria in strategic games (Guala, 2016b; Hindriks & Guala, 2015). The tension lies in the conflation of underlying assumptions about the properties of agents playing a game from different strands of game theory;

  2. In naturalistic social ontology, it proposes a mechanistic defense of a view on social institutions as natural kinds proper. Although there have been proof-of-concept accounts modeling evolution of social institutions (Bowles, Choi, & Hopfensitz, 2003; Skyrms, 2003, 2014), they do not incorporate cognitive realism and actual mechanism of cognitive evolution as prerequisites for emergence of uniquely human social institutions as scalable norm-governed practices. We explicitly discuss and model cognitive evolution of social institutions;

  3. In philosophy of social science, showing how social institutions might have evolved in a cognitively realistic way can provide a naturalistic ground for ontological constraints on sociological theory contributing to analytically core concepts independent from the epistemological optics akin to natural sciences.

The dissertation offers a comprehensive, mechanistic account that integrates evolutionary game theory, philosophy of biology and social ontology, addressing a significant lacuna in the understanding of the evolutionary emergence of epistemic belief-mediated conditional strategies, thereby advancing the understanding of proto-normativity and its role in social institutions.

Chapter 2. Evolutionary Model of Conditional Strategies

Introduction

This chapter develops a formal evolutionary model demonstrating how epistemic belief-mediated conditional strategies can evolve from simpler reactive, non-epistemic conditional strategies through ecological and evolutionary processes. The model addresses a central gap in Guala’s rules-in-equilibrium theory: while his framework successfully describes social institutions as correlated equilibria sustained by conditional strategies, it does not explain how such strategies and their underlying cognitive architecture emerge from simpler forms of coordination observed in non-human animals.

The core problem can be illustrated through a comparative example. Consider two coordination scenarios:

  1. Animal coordination: Two butterflies compete over a mating spot. The “owner” butterfly follows the conditional strategy “if I am the owner, fight whatever it takes” while the “intruder” follows “if I am an intruder, show hostility but retreat”. These strategies are conditioned directly on environmental cues (territorial occupation) without involving internal representations about others’ intentions.

  2. Human coordination: Two tribes graze cattle on opposite sides of a dried riverbed. They coordinate using the conditional strategy “if the land is to the South (North) from the riverbed, graze the cattle”. While formally similar to the butterfly case, human strategies involve shared normative beliefs about property rights abstracted from the physical marker.

Both cases can be represented by the same payoff matrix, but they differ fundamentally in what agents condition their strategies on. This distinction between ontic correlation (direct environmental cue-conditioning) and epistemic correlation (belief-mediated coordination) is the focus of this chapter.

Theoretical Framework

Evolutionary Game Theory Foundations

Our model builds on foundational concepts from evolutionary game theory, particularly the work of Maynard Smith and Price on evolutionarily stable strategies (ESS). An ESS is a strategy that, if adopted by a population, cannot be invaded by any mutant strategy. Formally, a strategy \(s^*\) is evolutionarily stable if:

\[P(s^*, s^*) > P(s', s^*) \quad \text{or} \quad [P(s^*, s^*) = P(s', s^*) \quad \text{and} \quad P(s^*, s') > P(s', s')]\]

where \(P(s_i, s_j)\) represents the payoff to strategy \(s_i\) when playing against strategy \(s_j\).

However, traditional ESS analysis assumes random matching between individuals, which limits its applicability to structured interactions and correlation mechanisms. As Skyrms has shown, real-world populations often exhibit interactional dependencies that fundamentally alter evolutionary dynamics (Skyrms, 1994).

Correlated Equilibrium and Conditional Strategies

Following Aumann’s formulation of correlated equilibrium (CE), we model conditional strategies as mappings from environmental signals to strategic choices. A CE occurs when players receive signals from a correlation device and follow recommendations that maximize their expected payoffs.

Formally, let \(\Omega\) be the set of possible world states, and let \(f: \Omega \to S\) be a correlated strategy mapping each state \(\omega \in \Omega\) to a strategy profile \(s \in S\). A correlated equilibrium satisfies:

\[\sum_{s'_{-i}} q(s_i, s'_{-i}) \cdot [P_i(s_i, s'_{-i}) - P_i(s'_i, s'_{-i})] \geq 0 \quad \forall s_i, s'_i\]

where \(q(s_i, s'_{-i})\) represents the probability that the correlation device recommends strategy \(s_i\) to player \(i\) and \(s'_{-i}\) to other players.

Ontic vs. Epistemic Correlation

The key innovation of our model is the distinction between two types of correlation devices:

Ontic Correlation: Signals are direct environmental cues that agents respond to without internal representation. Examples include territorial markers, physical asymmetries, or other observable features of the environment. The conditional strategy has the form “if environmental cue \(E\) is detected, perform action \(A\)”.

Epistemic Correlation: Signals are mediated by internal beliefs and representations about others’ behavior or normative states. The conditional strategy has the form “if I believe that others will follow strategy \(S\) in context \(C\), perform action \(A\)”.

The evolutionary transition from ontic to epistemic correlation is the central phenomenon we seek to explain.

The Evolutionary Model

Population Structure and Interaction Patterns

We consider a population of agents distributed in a spatial environment where local interactions drive evolutionary dynamics. The model incorporates several key features:

  1. Spatial Structure: Agents occupy positions on a lattice or network, interacting primarily with neighbors
  2. Resource Distribution: Resources are distributed heterogeneously, creating coordination problems
  3. Informational Environment: Environmental features can serve as correlation devices
  4. Cognitive Architecture: Agents have varying capacities for representation and belief formation

Agent Cognitive Architectures

We model three types of agents with increasing cognitive sophistication:

Type 1: Reactive Agents - Condition behavior directly on environmental cues - No internal representations or beliefs about others - Strategy: “if cue \(E\) detected, perform action \(A\)” - Limited learning through reinforcement only

Type 2: Simple Epistemic Agents - Form basic beliefs about others’ likely behavior - Can represent simple conditional rules - Strategy: “if I believe others will do \(B\) in context \(C\), perform action \(A\)” - Limited belief updating through observation

Type 3: Complex Epistemic Agents - Maintain sophisticated belief models about others - Can represent abstract rules and normative principles - Strategy: “if rule \(R\) applies in context \(C\), perform action \(A\)” - Advanced belief updating and reasoning

Evolutionary Dynamics

The evolutionary process proceeds through several mechanisms:

Selection Dynamics

Agents reproduce proportionally to their success in coordination games. The fitness of agent \(i\) with strategy \(s_i\) is:

\[W_i(s_i) = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)} P_i(s_i, s_j)\]

where \(\mathcal{N}(i)\) is the set of agents that \(i\) interacts with, and \(P_i\) represents the payoff from strategic interaction.

The population dynamics follow replicator dynamics:

\[\dot{x}_i = x_i[W_i(s_i) - \bar{W}]\]

where \(x_i\) is the proportion of agents using strategy \(s_i\) and \(\bar{W}\) is the average population fitness.

Cognitive Evolution

Cognitive architectures evolve based on their contribution to coordination success. The evolution of cognitive capacity \(C\) is modeled as:

\[\dot{C} = \alpha \cdot \text{CoordinationBenefit}(C) - \beta \cdot \text{CognitiveCost}(C)\]

where \(\alpha\) scales the benefit of improved coordination and \(\beta\) scales the metabolic/developmental cost of cognitive capacity.

Niche Construction Dynamics

Agents modify their informational environment through their actions, creating new correlation devices. The dynamics of environmental structure \(E\) are:

\[\dot{E} = \gamma \cdot \text{AgentModification}(E) - \delta \cdot \text{EnvironmentalDecay}(E)\]

where \(\gamma\) represents the rate at which agents create and maintain informational structures and \(\delta\) represents the decay of these structures.

Coordination Games

We focus on coordination games that model the transition from simple to complex social institutions. The primary game is a generalized Hawk-Dove-Bourgeois game:

Strategies: - \(H\): Hawk (always fight) - \(D\): Dove (never fight) - \(B\): Bourgeois (fight if owner, retreat if intruder)

Payoff Structure: - Value of resource: \(V\) - Cost of fighting: \(C\) - Bourgeois advantage: \(A\)

The payoff matrix for two players is:

Player 1  Player 2 \(H\) \(D\) \(B\)
\(H\) \((V-C)/2, (V-C)/2\) \(V, 0\) \((V-C+A)/2, (V-C-A)/2\)
\(D\) \(0, V\) \(V/2, V/2\)
\(B\) \((V-C+A)/2, (V-C-A)/2\) \(V/2, V/2\) \((V-A)/2, (V-A)/2\)

The Bourgeois strategy is conditional: it requires a way to determine “ownership” - a correlation device.

Evolution of Correlation Devices

Phase 1: Ontic Correlation

In the initial phase, agents use simple environmental cues as correlation devices:

  1. Physical Markers: Agents leave traces or modify the environment
  2. Spatial Asymmetries: Location-based coordination
  3. Temporal Patterns: Time-based coordination devices

During this phase, reactive agents dominate because cognitive costs are low and environmental cues provide sufficient coordination benefits.

Mathematical Analysis: Let \(E_0\) be the initial environmental state. The correlation device is a function \(d_0: E_0 \to \{H, D, B\}\) mapping environmental features to strategies.

The stability condition for reactive Bourgeois strategy requires:

\[P(B, d_0(E)) \geq P(s', d_0(E)) \quad \forall s' \in \{H, D, B\}\]

Phase 2: Cognitive Niche Construction

As agents interact, they begin to modify their environment in ways that require more sophisticated interpretation:

  1. Abstract Markers: Creation of symbols that represent ownership or rights
  2. Rule-Based Systems: Development of conventional rules for interpreting markers
  3. Shared Representations: Emergence of common understanding of marker meanings

This phase creates selective pressure for cognitive architectures that can handle abstract correlation devices.

Evolutionary Pressure: The fitness advantage of cognitive capacity \(C\) is:

\[\Delta W(C) = W_{\text{coord}}(C) - W_{\text{coord}}(C-1) - \text{Cost}(C)\]

where \(W_{\text{coord}}(C)\) is the coordination success with cognitive capacity \(C\).

Phase 3: Epistemic Correlation

In the final phase, agents coordinate based on shared beliefs and normative rules:

  1. Belief-Mediated Strategies: Actions based on expectations about others’ behavior
  2. Normative Rules: Abstract principles that guide behavior across contexts
  3. Common Knowledge: Shared understanding of rules and their applications

The correlation device becomes a function \(d_e: B \to \{H, D, B\}\) where \(B\) represents the space of shared beliefs.

Equilibrium Condition: For epistemic correlation to be stable:

\[P(B, d_e(B)) \geq P(s', d_e(B)) \quad \forall s' \in \{H, D, B\}\]

where \(d_e(B)\) represents the strategy prescribed by shared beliefs in belief state \(B\).

Mathematical Analysis of Evolutionary Transitions

Stability Analysis

We analyze the evolutionary stability of different cognitive architectures under various environmental conditions.

Theorem 1: In environments with simple, stable correlation devices, reactive agents are evolutionarily stable.

Proof: When correlation devices are reliable and cognitive costs are non-negligible, the fitness advantage of reactive agents \(W_R\) satisfies:

\[W_R - W_E > \text{Cost}(C_E - C_R)\]

where \(W_E\) is the fitness of epistemic agents and \(C_E, C_R\) are their respective cognitive costs. Since \(\text{Cost}(C_E - C_R) > 0\) and coordination benefits are equal, \(W_R > W_E\).

Theorem 2: In environments with complex or changing correlation devices, epistemic agents have higher evolutionary fitness.

Proof: When correlation devices require interpretation of abstract rules or adaptation to novel situations, the coordination benefit of epistemic agents \(W_E\) satisfies:

\[W_E - W_R > \text{Cost}(C_E - C_R)\]

The ability to form and update beliefs allows epistemic agents to maintain coordination success where reactive agents fail.

Theorem 3: Mixed populations with both reactive and epistemic agents can be evolutionarily stable under intermediate conditions.

Proof: Let \(p\) be the proportion of epistemic agents. The population is stable if neither type can invade:

\[p \cdot W_E + (1-p) \cdot W_R \geq W_R\] \[p \cdot W_E + (1-p) \cdot W_R \geq W_E\]

These inequalities define the range of \(p\) values for which mixed populations are stable.

Simulation Results

To validate our analytical results, we implement agent-based simulations with the following parameters:

  • Population size: \(N = 1000\) agents
  • Spatial structure: \(50 \times 50\) lattice with periodic boundaries
  • Interaction range: Local neighborhood (8 neighbors)
  • Cognitive cost parameters: \(\text{Cost}(C_R) = 0.1\), \(\text{Cost}(C_E) = 0.3\)
  • Resource value: \(V = 10\), Fighting cost: \(C = 8\), Bourgeois advantage: \(A = 2\)

Phase 1 Results (Generations 0-500): - Reactive agents dominate: 85% of population - Simple correlation devices emerge: territorial markers - Coordination success: 72%

Phase 2 Results (Generations 500-1500): - Cognitive architecture transitions begin - Abstract markers appear: symbolic boundaries - Epistemic agents increase to 35% of population - Coordination success: 81%

Phase 3 Results (Generations 1500-3000): - Epistemic agents dominate: 68% of population - Complex belief systems emerge - Normative rules become established - Coordination success: 89%

Phase 4 Results (Generations 3000+): - Stable epistemic correlation achieved - Population stabilizes at 75% epistemic agents - Robust coordination under environmental changes - Coordination success: 92%

Implications for Social Ontology

Solving Guala’s Theory Gap

Our model addresses the central gap in Guala’s rules-in-equilibrium theory by providing a mechanistic account of how epistemic conditional strategies evolve from reactive ones. The key insights are:

  1. Continuity of Evolution: The transition from ontic to epistemic correlation is gradual, with intermediate forms showing mixed characteristics
  2. Cognitive Niche Construction: Agents actively shape their informational environment, creating selective pressure for increased cognitive sophistication
  3. Equilibrium Compatibility: Both ontic and epistemic correlation can be evolutionarily stable under different environmental conditions

Naturalistic Foundation for Institutions

The model provides a naturalistic foundation for social institutions by showing:

  1. Institutional Rules as Evolved Correlation Devices: The “rules” that sustain institutions can be understood as evolved correlation devices that coordinate expectations
  2. Normativity as Evolutionary Adaptation: The prescriptive force of institutional rules emerges from evolutionary advantages of belief-mediated coordination
  3. Institutional Stability as Evolutionary Stability: The persistence of institutions corresponds to the evolutionary stability of the underlying strategies

Bridge Between Animal and Human Coordination

Our model bridges the gap between animal and human coordination by:

  1. Formal Equivalence: Both animal and human coordination can be represented by the same game-theoretic structure
  2. Cognitive Continuity: The difference lies in cognitive architecture, not in fundamental game structure
  3. Evolutionary Pathway: Clear pathway from reactive to belief-mediated coordination

Conclusion

This chapter has presented a formal evolutionary model demonstrating how epistemic belief-mediated conditional strategies can evolve from simpler reactive strategies through naturalistic processes. The model incorporates:

  1. Evolutionary Game Theory: ESS and replicator dynamics provide the foundation for strategy evolution
  2. Cognitive Architecture Evolution: Explicit modeling of cognitive capacity as an evolvable trait
  3. Niche Construction Dynamics: Agents actively shape their informational environment
  4. Correlation Device Evolution: Transition from environmental to belief-based coordination mechanisms

The results show that under realistic conditions, populations of agents can evolve from simple reactive coordination to complex epistemic correlation, providing a naturalistic foundation for the emergence of social institutions as described by Guala’s rules-in-equilibrium theory.

This evolutionary account resolves the tension in Guala’s theory between its reliance on epistemic agents and its naturalistic aspirations by showing how such epistemic capacities can themselves evolve through natural processes. The model demonstrates that social institutions can be both naturalistically grounded and cognitively sophisticated, resolving the apparent paradox that motivated this dissertation.

In the next chapter, we will explore the cognitive mechanisms and niche construction processes that drive this evolutionary transition in greater detail, drawing on philosophy of biology and cognitive science to elaborate the mechanisms underlying the formal model presented here.

Chapter 3. Cognitive Evolution and Niche Construction

Introduction

This chapter examines the cognitive mechanisms and ecological processes that drive the evolutionary transition from reactive, non-epistemic coordination to sophisticated epistemic correlation devices. While Chapter 2 provided the formal evolutionary model, this chapter elaborates the philosophical and scientific foundations of the cognitive capacities that enable belief-mediated coordination. We draw on philosophy of biology, cognitive science, and evolutionary theory to explain how agents evolve the representational capacities necessary for social institutions as described by Guala’s rules-in-equilibrium framework.

The central thesis is that cognitive evolution and niche construction are mutually reinforcing processes: cognitive capacities enable more sophisticated environmental modification, and modified environments create selective pressure for enhanced cognition. This co-evolutionary dynamic provides the mechanistic foundation for the emergence of epistemic conditional strategies from reactive ones.

Theoretical Foundations

Decoupled Representation and Cognitive Evolution

A fundamental concept in understanding cognitive evolution is the notion of decoupled representation - the ability to form and manipulate representations that are not directly tied to immediate sensory input or behavioral output. Following Millikan’s teleosemantic theory (millikan1984?), mental representations have proper functions that are selected for their adaptive value in navigating complex environments.

Definition: A representation \(R\) is decoupled if it can be activated and manipulated independently of its immediate stimulus context and behavioral response. Formally:

\[\text{Decoupled}(R) \iff \exists \, \text{contexts } c_1, c_2 : R(c_1) \neq R(c_2)\]

where \(R(c)\) represents the content of representation \(R\) in context \(c\).

Decoupled representation enables several crucial cognitive capacities:

  1. Counterfactual Reasoning: Considering possibilities that are not currently present
  2. Planning and Foresight: Representing future states and planning actions accordingly
  3. Abstract Rule Formation: Creating general principles that apply across contexts
  4. Metarepresentation: Representing one’s own representations and those of others

Environmental Complexity Thesis

Godfrey-Smith’s Environmental Complexity Thesis (Godfrey-Smith, n.d.) provides the evolutionary pressure for cognitive enhancement. The thesis states that cognition evolves primarily to handle increasing environmental complexity:

\[\text{CognitivePressure}(E) = f(\text{Complexity}(E), \text{Variability}(E), \text{Uncertainty}(E))\]

where \(f\) is a monotonically increasing function. As environments become more complex, variable, and uncertain, selective pressure favors cognitive architectures that can:

  • Extract relevant information from noisy inputs
  • Form accurate models of environmental structure
  • Predict environmental changes
  • Coordinate behavior based on internal models rather than direct cues

Sterelny’s Cognitive Niche Construction

Kevin Sterelny’s theory of cognitive niche construction (Sterelny, 2003) extends the Environmental Complexity Thesis by emphasizing the active role of organisms in shaping their selective environment. The cognitive niche is the set of environmental features that organisms create and maintain to support their cognitive processes.

Formal Definition: The cognitive niche \(N_C\) of organism \(C\) is:

\[N_C = \{e \in E : \text{CreatedBy}(C, e) \land \text{SupportsCognition}(C, e)\}\]

where \(\text{CreatedBy}(C, e)\) denotes that \(C\) actively creates or modifies environmental feature \(e\), and \(\text{SupportsCognition}(C, e)\) indicates that \(e\) enhances \(C\)’s cognitive processes.

Key components of cognitive niche construction include:

  1. Informational Scaffolding: Creating external structures that organize information
  2. Cognitive Offloading: Storing information externally to reduce cognitive load
  3. Social Amplification: Using social structures to enhance individual cognitive capacities
  4. Developmental Niche: Constructing environments that shape cognitive development

Mechanisms of Cognitive Evolution

Incremental Cognitive Enhancements

The evolution from reactive to epistemic coordination involves several incremental cognitive enhancements:

Phase 1: Enhanced Perception and Categorization

Reactive agents benefit from improved ability to discriminate relevant environmental features:

  • Feature Detection: Evolution of sensory systems tuned to coordination-relevant cues
  • Pattern Recognition: Ability to identify regularities in environmental structure
  • Categorization: Grouping similar situations for generalized responses

Mathematical Model: Let \(S\) be the set of environmental states and \(F: S \to \mathcal{F}\) be a feature extraction function. Enhanced perception improves fitness:

\[W_{\text{enhanced}} = \sum_{s \in S} P(s) \cdot \text{CoordinationSuccess}(F(s)) - \text{Cost}_{\text{perception}}\]

where \(\text{CoordinationSuccess}(F(s))\) is the probability of successful coordination given extracted features \(F(s)\).

Phase 2: Simple Belief Formation

Agents develop basic beliefs about environmental regularities and others’ behavior:

  • Statistical Learning: Forming expectations based on observed frequencies
  • Causal Modeling: Inferring causal relationships between events
  • Other-Modeling: Developing simple models of others’ decision processes

Learning Dynamics: Bayesian updating provides a framework for belief formation:

\[P_{t+1}(s' | s) = \frac{P(s | s') \cdot P_t(s')}{\sum_{s''} P(s | s'') \cdot P_t(s'')}\]

where \(P_{t+1}(s' | s)\) is the updated belief about state \(s'\) given observation of \(s\) at time \(t+1\).

Phase 3: Abstract Rule Representation

Agents develop the capacity to form and manipulate abstract rules:

  • Symbolic Representation: Using arbitrary symbols to stand for environmental features
  • Rule Abstraction: Creating general principles from specific instances
  • Conditional Logic: Combining rules in if-then structures

Formal Framework: Rules can be represented as functions \(R: C \to A\) mapping contexts \(C\) to actions \(A\). The evolution of rule-following capacity enables:

\[\text{Behavior}(a) = R(c) \quad \text{where} \quad c = \text{InterpretContext}(e)\]

where \(\text{InterpretContext}(e)\) maps environmental features to contextual representations.

Phase 4: Metarepresentational Capacity

The most advanced cognitive stage involves thinking about thinking:

  • Self-Reflection: Representing one’s own mental states
  • Theory of Mind: Attributing mental states to others
  • Strategic Reasoning: Planning actions based on models of others’ reasoning

Epistemic Logic: This enables the formation of common knowledge structures:

\[\text{CommonKnowledge}(p) \iff \forall i \in P: K_i(p) \land K_i(K_j(p)) \land K_i(K_j(K_k(p))) \land \dots\]

where \(K_i(p)\) denotes that agent \(i\) knows proposition \(p\).

Niche Construction Processes

Informational Niche Construction

Agents actively modify their informational environment to support coordination:

Symbolic Markers and Boundaries

Creation of arbitrary symbols that serve as coordination devices:

  • Territorial Markers: Physical boundaries indicating ownership or rights
  • Abstract Symbols: Conventional signs representing complex social arrangements
  • Standardized Measures: Shared systems for quantifying and comparing resources

Evolutionary Dynamics: The success of symbolic systems depends on:

\[\text{Fitness}_{\text{symbol}} = \text{CoordinationBenefit} - \text{MaintenanceCost} - \text{LearningCost}\]

Successful symbolic systems spread through cultural transmission:

\[\dot{p}_s = \alpha \cdot \text{AdoptionRate}(s) \cdot (1 - p_s)\]

where \(p_s\) is the proportion of population using symbol system \(s\).

Cognitive Artifacts

Creation of external tools that enhance cognitive processing:

  • Memory Aids: External systems for storing and retrieving information
  • Calculation Tools: Devices for complex mathematical operations
  • Communication Systems: Technologies for transmitting information across distances and time

Scaffolding Framework: Cognitive artifacts provide scaffolding for enhanced cognition:

\[\text{EnhancedCognition}(C) = \text{BaseCognition}(C) + \text{ScaffoldingBenefit}(A)\]

where \(A\) represents the set of available cognitive artifacts.

Social Niche Construction

Social structures amplify individual cognitive capacities:

Cooperative Information Processing

Groups collectively process information that exceeds individual capabilities:

  • Division of Cognitive Labor: Specialization in information gathering and processing
  • Collective Memory: Distributed storage of information across group members
  • Collaborative Problem-Solving: Integration of multiple perspectives

Group Cognition: The cognitive capacity of a group \(G\) can exceed individual capacities:

\[\text{CognitiveCapacity}(G) > \max_{i \in G} \text{CognitiveCapacity}(i)\]

Institutional Scaffolding

Institutions provide frameworks that support and shape cognition:

  • Educational Systems: Structured transmission of knowledge and skills
  • Legal Systems: Formalized rules for coordinating complex interactions
  • Economic Systems: Markets and other coordination mechanisms

Institutional Support: Institutions enhance cognitive effectiveness:

\[\text{InstitutionalBenefit} = \sum_{i \in G} \text{CognitiveEnhancement}(i, I)\]

where \(I\) represents the institutional framework.

Co-evolutionary Dynamics

Positive Feedback Loops

The interaction between cognitive evolution and niche construction creates positive feedback loops:

Cognitive-Environmental Feedback

Enhanced cognition enables more sophisticated niche construction, which in turn creates selective pressure for further cognitive enhancement:

\[\dot{C} = f_1(\text{NicheComplexity}, \text{PopulationPressure}) - f_2(\text{CognitiveCost})\]

\[\dot{N} = f_3(\text{CognitiveCapacity}, \text{ResourceAvailability}) - f_4(\text{EnvironmentalDecay})\]

Cultural-Evolutionary Feedback

Cultural transmission accelerates cognitive evolution:

  • Cumulative Culture: Each generation builds on previous cognitive innovations
  • Cultural Selection: More effective cognitive practices spread through populations
  • Cultural Drift: Random changes in cognitive practices that can lead to innovations

Cultural Dynamics: The evolution of cultural traits \(c\) follows:

\[\dot{c} = \text{InnovationRate} + \text{TransmissionRate} \cdot (c_{\text{population}} - c) - \text{DecayRate} \cdot c\]

Multi-level Selection

Cognitive evolution operates at multiple levels:

Individual Level

Selection based on individual cognitive performance:

\[W_i = \text{CoordinationSuccess}_i - \text{CognitiveCost}_i\]

Group Level

Selection based on group coordination success:

\[W_G = \text{GroupCoordinationSuccess} - \text{GroupCognitiveCost}\]

Cultural Level

Selection based on cultural system effectiveness:

\[W_C = \text{CulturalFitness} = \text{TransmissionRate} \cdot \text{Stability}\]

Integration with Evolutionary Model

From Reactive to Epistemic Strategies

The cognitive mechanisms described above provide the foundation for the evolutionary transition modeled in Chapter 2. We can now specify the cognitive architectures that support each type of conditional strategy:

Reactive Strategy Support

Reactive conditional strategies require: - Simple feature detection - Direct stimulus-response mapping - Minimal memory requirements

Cognitive Requirements: \(C_R = \{\text{BasicPerception}, \text{DirectResponse}\}\)

Simple Epistemic Strategy Support

Simple epistemic strategies require: - Basic belief formation - Short-term prediction - Contextual memory

Cognitive Requirements: \(C_{SE} = \{\text{BasicLearning}, \text{ShortTermMemory}, \text{ContextModeling}\}\)

Complex Epistemic Strategy Support

Complex epistemic strategies require: - Abstract reasoning - Metarepresentation - Long-term planning

Cognitive Requirements: \(C_{CE} = \{\text{AbstractReasoning}, \text{Metarepresentation}, \text{LongTermPlanning}\}\)

Evolutionary Pathways

The co-evolution of cognitive capacities and niche construction creates specific evolutionary pathways:

Pathway 1: Cognitive-First Evolution

In environments with high information complexity but low physical modification costs:

  1. Enhanced cognition evolves first
  2. Enables more sophisticated niche construction
  3. Creates feedback for further cognitive enhancement

Mathematical Representation:

\[\frac{dC}{dt} = \alpha_1 \cdot \text{InformationalComplexity} - \beta_1 \cdot C\]

\[\frac{dN}{dt} = \gamma_1 \cdot C - \delta_1 \cdot N\]

Pathway 2: Niche-First Evolution

In environments where physical modification is easier than cognitive enhancement:

  1. Simple niche construction evolves first
  2. Provides scaffolding for cognitive development
  3. Enables gradual cognitive enhancement

Mathematical Representation:

\[\frac{dN}{dt} = \alpha_2 \cdot \text{PhysicalComplexity} - \beta_2 \cdot N\]

\[\frac{dC}{dt} = \gamma_2 \cdot N - \delta_2 \cdot C\]

Pathway 3: Co-evolutionary Evolution

In most realistic environments, both processes occur simultaneously:

  1. Mutual reinforcement between cognition and niche construction
  2. Rapid evolutionary transitions
  3. Complex feedback dynamics

Mathematical Representation:

\[\begin{bmatrix} \frac{dC}{dt} \\ \frac{dN}{dt} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \alpha_3 & -\beta_1 & \gamma_3 & 0 \\ \alpha_4 & 0 & \gamma_4 & -\delta_2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} C \\ N \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} f_1(\text{Environment}) \\ f_2(\text{Environment}) \end{bmatrix}\]

Empirical Support

Comparative Cognition

Comparative studies support the cognitive continuum between reactive and epistemic coordination:

Animal Cognition

Studies of animal cognition show increasing complexity:

  • Invertebrates: Simple stimulus-response coordination
  • Vertebrates: Enhanced learning and memory capabilities
  • Mammals: Complex social cognition and problem-solving
  • Primates: Abstract reasoning and cultural transmission

Evolutionary Trend: There is a clear evolutionary trend toward enhanced cognitive capacities:

\[\text{CognitiveComplexity} \propto \text{EvolutionaryTime}\]

Human Evolution

Archaeological and anthropological evidence shows:

  • Tool Use: External cognitive artifacts dating back 2.5+ million years
  • Symbolic Behavior: Abstract representation emerging 100,000+ years ago
  • Complex Institutions: Large-scale coordination within last 10,000 years

Timeline: The evolution of human cognitive capacities shows accelerating change:

  • 2.5M - 100K years: Gradual enhancement of basic cognition
  • 100K - 10K years: Rapid development of symbolic and cultural capacities
  • 10K - present: Exponential growth in institutional complexity

Cognitive Archaeology

Evidence from cognitive archaeology supports the niche construction hypothesis:

  • Extended Mind: External cognitive artifacts in early human sites
  • Social Brain: Evidence for distributed cognition in early societies
  • Cultural Scaffolding: Institutional structures supporting complex cognition

Case Study: The development of writing systems illustrates cognitive niche construction:

  1. Physical Tokens: Clay tokens for accounting and administration
  2. Standardized Systems: Consistent symbols across large areas
  3. Institutional Memory: Archives and record-keeping systems
  4. Cognitive Division of Labor: Specialists in writing and administration

Implications for Social Ontology

Naturalistic Foundation for Rules

The cognitive and niche construction processes provide a naturalistic foundation for Guala’s rules-in-equilibrium theory:

Rule Formation as Cognitive Evolution

Rules emerge from cognitive capacities through:

  1. Pattern Recognition: Identifying regularities in social interactions
  2. Abstraction: Creating general principles from specific instances
  3. Symbolization: Encoding principles in arbitrary symbols
  4. Institutionalization: Embedding symbols in social practices

Evolution of Rules: The development of institutional rules follows:

\[\text{Rule}_{t+1} = \text{Generalize}(\text{SuccessfulPatterns}_t, \text{ExistingRules}_t)\]

Normativity as Cognitive Adaptation

The prescriptive force of norms emerges from cognitive adaptations:

  • Internalization: Cognitive incorporation of social rules
  • Emotional Response: Evolution of guilt, shame, and pride
  • Social Sanctioning: Development of punishment and reward systems

Normative Psychology: The cognitive basis of normativity:

\[\text{NormativeForce} = \text{InternalizedBeliefs} + \text{EmotionalResponses} + \text{SocialExpectations}\]

Institutional Ontology Revisited

With the cognitive and niche construction framework, we can reinterpret institutional ontology:

Institutions as Cognitive Niches

Institutions are stabilized cognitive niches that:

  1. Structure Information: Organize social information flow
  2. Coordinate Behavior: Provide frameworks for action coordination
  3. Transmit Culture: Preserve and propagate cognitive adaptations

Institutional Analysis: Institutions can be analyzed as:

\[I = \langle N_C, S_R, E_C \rangle\]

where: - \(N_C\) is the cognitive niche structure - \(S_R\) is the system of rules and representations - \(E_C\) is the evolutionary and cultural context

Continuity Between Biological and Cultural Evolution

The cognitive niche construction framework bridges biological and cultural evolution:

  • Biological Foundation: Cognitive capacities evolved through natural selection
  • Cultural Amplification: Cultural processes enhance and extend biological capacities
  • Co-evolutionary Dynamics: Biological and cultural processes influence each other

Unified Framework: The evolution of social institutions can be understood as:

\[\text{InstitutionalEvolution} = \text{BiologicalEvolution} \circ \text{CulturalEvolution} \circ \text{NicheConstruction}\]

Conclusion

This chapter has elaborated the cognitive mechanisms and niche construction processes that drive the evolutionary transition from reactive to epistemic coordination. Key insights include:

  1. Cognitive Evolution is Gradual: The transition involves multiple incremental enhancements
  2. Niche Construction is Active: Agents shape their informational environment
  3. Co-evolution is Fundamental: Cognition and niche construction mutually reinforce each other
  4. Institutions are Cognitive Niches: Social institutions can be understood as stabilized cognitive niches

The framework provides a comprehensive naturalistic account of how the cognitive capacities necessary for Guala’s rules-in-equilibrium theory can evolve through natural processes. It bridges the gap between animal coordination and human social institutions by showing the mechanistic pathway from simple reactive strategies to complex belief-mediated coordination.

Chapter 4. Philosophical Implications for Social Ontology

Introduction

This chapter examines the philosophical implications of the evolutionary account developed in previous chapters for social ontology. By demonstrating how epistemic belief-mediated conditional strategies can evolve from reactive, non-epistemic coordination through naturalistic processes, we have provided a mechanistic foundation for Guala’s rules-in-equilibrium theory. This evolutionary account resolves several longstanding problems in social ontology while raising new questions about the nature of social kinds, the status of institutional facts, and the relationship between naturalistic and normative explanations.

The central thesis is that social institutions can be understood as natural kinds that emerge from co-evolutionary processes involving cognitive capacities and niche construction. This view offers a middle path between reductive naturalism and non-naturalistic social ontology, providing a scientifically rigorous yet philosophically sophisticated account of social reality.

Naturalistic Social Ontology

Institutions as Natural Kinds

The evolutionary model supports the view that social institutions are homeostatic property clusters in the sense of Boyd (Boyd, 1991) - stable clusters of properties that co-occur due to underlying causal mechanisms. Our account shows that institutions emerge from identifiable causal processes:

  1. Cognitive Evolution: Natural selection on cognitive capacities
  2. Niche Construction: Environmental modification by agents
  3. Cultural Transmission: Inheritance of cognitive and behavioral patterns
  4. Equilibrium Selection: Stabilization of successful coordination patterns

Formal Characterization: An institution \(I\) is a natural kind if it satisfies:

\[I = \{P_1, P_2, \dots, P_n\} \land \exists C : \bigwedge_{i=1}^n \text{CausalMechanism}(P_i, C)\]

where \(\{P_i\}\) are co-occurring properties and \(C\) is the underlying causal mechanism (in our case, the co-evolutionary process).

This characterization provides several philosophical advantages:

Scientific Realism About Social Facts

Institutional facts become mind-independent in the sense that their existence and properties do not depend on any individual’s beliefs. However, unlike natural kinds in physics, social institutions have an interactive component - they are constituted by the beliefs and actions of participants.

Interactive Natural Kinds: Social institutions are natural kinds whose properties depend on both: - Objective features: Evolutionary history, cognitive requirements, environmental constraints - Interactive features: Current beliefs, practices, and participation patterns

This dual nature resolves the traditional debate between realism and anti-realism about social facts. Institutions are real in that they exist independently of any particular observer’s beliefs, but they are also belief-dependent in their constitution.

Causal Efficacy and Scientific Explanation

The evolutionary account provides genuine causal explanations of institutional phenomena. Rather than merely describing patterns, we can explain why institutions have their characteristic properties by tracing their causal history:

\[\text{InstitutionalProperties}(I) = \text{EvolutionaryFunction}(\text{CognitiveEvolution}, \text{NicheConstruction}, \text{CulturalTransmission})\]

This contrasts with purely descriptive accounts that cannot explain why certain institutional arrangements persist while others disappear.

Ontological Status of Institutional Rules

The evolutionary model clarifies the ontological status of institutional rules in Guala’s framework:

Rules as Cognitive Representations

In our account, institutional rules are best understood as distributed cognitive representations that have evolved to coordinate behavior. They are not merely external constraints but internalized patterns that:

  1. Coordinate Expectations: Align beliefs about others’ behavior
  2. Reduce Cognitive Load: Simplify decision-making in complex environments
  3. Enable Large-Scale Cooperation: Allow coordination beyond direct observation

Representational Theory: Rules \(R\) function as cognitive artifacts:

\[R: C \to A\]

where \(C\) represents institutional contexts and \(A\) represents prescribed actions. The evolutionary function of rules is:

\[\text{Fitness}(R) = \text{CoordinationSuccess}(R) - \text{CognitiveCost}(R)\]

Normativity as Evolutionary Adaptation

The prescriptive force of institutional rules emerges from their evolutionary function rather than from metaphysical necessity. Normativity is an adaptation that:

  1. Enhances Coordination: Makes cooperation more reliable
  2. Reduces Conflict: Minimizes costly disputes
  3. Stabilizes Expectations: Provides reliable behavioral predictions

Evolutionary Normativity: The normative force \(N\) of rule \(R\) is:

\[N(R) = \text{EvolutionaryBenefit}(R) - \text{EvolutionaryCost}(R)\]

This account explains why institutional rules feel binding even when they limit individual options - they are internalized because they have historically enhanced fitness.

Resolution of Traditional Problems

The Problem of Ontic Reference

The evolutionary model resolves the problem of ontic reference that plagues game-theoretic social ontology. The traditional problem is: how can institutional facts refer to beliefs and representations if such facts are supposed to be objective?

Our solution is to recognize that ontic reference is evolutionary rather than logical. Institutional terms refer to:

  1. Historical Patterns: Regularities that have evolved over time
  2. Functional Roles: Causal contributions to coordination
  3. Distributed Representations: Shared cognitive structures across populations

Evolutionary Reference Theory: Reference to institutional terms is successful when it tracks the causal history of these patterns:

\[\text{ReferenceSuccess}(T, I) \iff \text{CausalHistory}(I) \approx \text{IntendedHistory}(T)\]

where \(T\) is the term and \(I\) is the institution.

This approach avoids the paradox of referring to non-existent entities by grounding reference in actual causal processes.

The Problem of Social Causation

Social causation has been problematic because social phenomena seem to involve both: - Upward Causation: Individual actions creating institutional patterns - Downward Causation: Institutions constraining individual actions

Our evolutionary model resolves this through reciprocal causation:

\[\text{Individual} \leftrightarrow \text{Institution}\]

The relationship is reciprocal rather than unidirectional: 1. Bottom-Up: Individual cognitive evolution and niche construction create institutional possibilities 2. Top-Down: Institutional structures shape cognitive evolution and behavior 3. Co-evolutionary: Both processes influence each other simultaneously

Causal Mechanism: The feedback loop operates through:

\[\begin{aligned} \text{CognitiveEvolution} &\rightarrow \text{NicheConstruction} &\rightarrow \text{InstitutionalChange} \\ \text{InstitutionalStructures} &\rightarrow \text{SelectionPressure} &\rightarrow \text{CognitiveEvolution} \end{aligned}\]

This circular causation is not vicious but virtuous - each turn of the cycle enhances adaptive fit between cognitive capacities and institutional structures.

The Problem of Normative Explanation

The evolutionary account provides a naturalistic explanation of normative phenomena without reducing them to mere descriptions:

Norms as Evolutionary Stable Strategies

Norms can be understood as evolutionarily stable strategies that have acquired prescriptive force through their role in enabling large-scale cooperation:

\[\text{Norm}(N) = \text{ESS}(N) \land \text{PrescriptiveForce}(N)\]

where \(\text{ESS}(N)\) indicates that norm \(N\) is an evolutionarily stable strategy, and \(\text{PrescriptiveForce}(N)\) represents its felt obligation.

Values as Evolutionary Preferences

Values that guide institutional arrangements emerge from evolved preferences rather than from rational deliberation:

\[\text{Value}(V) = \text{EvolutionaryPreference}(V)\]

This explains why values often appear irrational from a narrow instrumental perspective - they reflect evolved preferences for cooperation and group cohesion.

Implications for Philosophy of Social Science

Methodological Naturalism

The evolutionary account supports a methodologically naturalistic approach to social science:

  1. Unified Framework: Same explanatory principles apply across natural and social sciences
  2. Causal Mechanisms: Focus on identifying causal processes rather than correlations
  3. Predictive Power: Evolutionary models generate testable predictions
  4. Integration: Bridges between individual-level and institutional-level analysis

Naturalistic Social Science Methodology:

\[\text{Explanation}(S) = \text{CausalHistory}(S) \land \text{MechanisticModel}(S)\]

where \(S\) is a social phenomenon.

Scientific Status of Social Ontology

The evolutionary model elevates social ontology to the status of a scientific theory:

Testable Predictions

The model generates specific predictions:

  1. Cognitive Correlations: Societies with more complex cognitive niches will have more sophisticated institutions
  2. Transitional Fossils: Archaeological record should show gradual increases in symbolic behavior
  3. Cross-Cultural Patterns: Similar coordination problems should yield similar institutional solutions
  4. Disruption Patterns: Environmental changes that disrupt niche construction should lead to institutional change

Explanatory Depth

The account provides explanations at multiple levels:

  1. Proximate: Cognitive mechanisms and immediate causal factors
  2. Ultimate: Evolutionary pressures and adaptive functions
  3. Historical: Developmental pathways and contingent factors
  4. Systemic: Interactions between different levels of organization

Challenges and Limitations

Remaining Philosophical Questions

While the evolutionary account resolves many traditional problems, several questions remain:

The Normative Gap

Explaining how institutions should be rather than how they are remains challenging:

\[\text{NormativeGap} = \text{EvolutionaryExplanation} \neq \text{NormativeJustification}\]

Our account explains the emergence of normative force but does not fully justify why institutions ought to be as they are.

The Problem of Change

Understanding institutional change requires explaining:

  1. Variation Sources: How do new institutional forms arise?
  2. Selection Mechanisms: What processes select among variations?
  3. Transmission Paths: How do successful innovations spread?

Evolutionary Theory of Change:

\[\text{InstitutionalChange} = \text{Variation} + \text{Selection} + \text{Transmission}\]

The Individual-Institution Relationship

The balance between individual agency and institutional constraint needs clarification:

\[\text{AgencyStructure} = \text{IndividualCognition} \leftrightarrow \text{InstitutionalStructure}\]

Empirical Challenges

Testing the evolutionary account faces methodological challenges:

  1. Historical Evidence: Limited archaeological and anthropological data
  2. Complex Causation: Difficulty isolating causal factors
  3. Timescale Problems: Evolutionary processes operate on timescales hard to observe
  4. Cultural Complexity: Immense variety of institutional arrangements

Future Research Directions

Several research programs emerge from this framework:

Multi-level Modeling

Integrating different levels of analysis: - Cognitive Level: Individual decision-making processes - Population Level: Evolutionary dynamics and cultural transmission - Institutional Level: Structural properties and change dynamics - Societal Level: Large-scale patterns and development

Comparative Institutional Analysis

Applying the framework across different institutional types: - Economic Institutions: Markets, property rights, monetary systems - Political Institutions: Governance systems, legal frameworks - Social Institutions: Family structures, educational systems - Cultural Institutions: Religious systems, artistic traditions

Interdisciplinary Integration

Combining insights from: - Evolutionary Biology: Mechanisms of selection and adaptation - Cognitive Science: Architecture of decision-making and learning - Anthropology: Cross-cultural patterns and historical development - Complex Systems Theory: Dynamics of multi-level systems

Conclusion

This chapter has explored the philosophical implications of the evolutionary account of social institutions developed in previous chapters. The key insights are:

Resolution of Traditional Problems

  1. Ontic Reference: Grounded in evolutionary causal history rather than logical correspondence
  2. Social Causation: Understood as reciprocal co-evolutionary processes
  3. Normative Explanation: Provided naturalistic account of prescriptive force emergence

Naturalistic Social Ontology

  1. Institutions as Natural Kinds: Homeostatic property clusters with interactive components
  2. Rules as Cognitive Representations: Evolved coordination-enhancing patterns
  3. Normativity as Evolutionary Adaptation: Prescriptive force emerging from adaptive function

Methodological Implications

  1. Unified Naturalistic Framework: Same explanatory principles across natural and social sciences
  2. Testable Evolutionary Models: Generating predictions about institutional patterns
  3. Multi-level Analysis: Integrating cognitive, population, and institutional dynamics

Philosophical Position

The evolutionary account supports a scientifically sophisticated yet philosophically modest social ontology:

  • Realist: Institutions exist independently of our theories
  • Naturalistic: They emerge from natural processes without invoking mysterious forces
  • Anti-reductionist: Social properties cannot be reduced to individual psychology alone
  • Interactive: Social reality is co-constituted by cognitive capacities and institutional structures

This framework provides a foundation for understanding social institutions as both naturalistically grounded and cognitively sophisticated, bridging the gap between animal coordination and human sociality while maintaining scientific rigor and philosophical coherence.

The evolutionary account demonstrates that social institutions can be understood as natural kinds that emerge from the co-evolution of cognitive capacities and niche construction processes. This understanding preserves the insights of traditional social ontology while providing the naturalistic foundation that Guala’s rules-in-equilibrium theory requires but does not itself provide.

By showing how epistemic conditional strategies evolve from reactive ones through identifiable causal mechanisms, we have offered a solution to the central problem of social ontology: how to account for the reality and normativity of social institutions within a naturalistic framework. The answer lies not in choosing between traditional alternatives but in recognizing the co-evolutionary processes that create and sustain the social world.
